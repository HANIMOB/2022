About

Movement Ecology is the discipline studying the spatiotemporal patterns and processes that regulate the animal movement, defined as a change in the spatial location of an individual in time. Ecologists benefit from ongoing advances in telemetry tools (namely, bio-logging) to collect accurate movement data and, from these, build spatial trajectories, i.e. sequences of temporally correlated observations describing the movement of an animal in space. These trajectories are then typically combined with remote sensing derived data, and/or with empirically collected data, to build models that can describe the determinants of animal movement, such as environmental constraints (e.g. snow layer or habitat fragmentation) or the inner status of individuals (e.g. memory, orientation capacity).

Animal movement and behavior are particularly altered by human activity and presence. So far, ecologists have been mainly devoted to understand the impacts on animal movement caused by human-induced environmental modifications (e.g. habitat loss and fragmentation), that are typically accounted for by static data that are readily available due to advances in remote sensing (e.g. road maps, high resolution forest cover,…). On the contrary, the impact of human presence (e.g. road traffic, human recreational activities,…), that would benefit from the association of animal movement data with direct, dynamic measures of human presence, has received much less attention. Indeed, ecologist capability to uncover the mechanisms underpinning the responses of wildlife to anthropogenic activity has been limited by the types and spatiotemporal resolution of human activity data. It is now time to fill this gap, which calls for an interdisciplinary effort finalized to the implementation of dynamic metrics of anthropogenic activity, or human mobility data, in animal movement analysis.
